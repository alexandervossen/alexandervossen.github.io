Known for being different? Reputation, Status, and the Optimal Distinctiveness of New Ventures

Alexander Vossen, Jonas Janisch
July 2022
Cite Project
Image credit: Slidebean on Unsplash
Type
Conference paper
Publication
VHB 2022 Annual Meeting

We investigate how audience evaluation of new ventures’ distinctiveness changes over time, i.e., when audiences become more familiar and less uncertain about them.Using longitudinal data on 4,279 products offered by 853 new ventures over 211 weeks on Amazon Launchpad, we observe familiarity through status, i.e., new ventures increasing establishment in the respective product category, and uncertainty through their reputation, i.e., the increasingly available evidence on past performance. Building on the notion of optimal distinctiveness as the trade-off between legitimacy and differentiation, we propose that both status and reputation steepen the U-shaped effect of new ventures’ distinctiveness. However, while highly distinct ventures benefit the most, new ventures face an ever increasing “stuck in the middle” problem that becomes particularly problematic when audiences become more familiar and less uncertain about averagely distinct ventures. Our results show that audience evaluation of a new venture’s distinctiveness is not only contingent on preferences or competitive and categorical contexts, but also by evolving degrees of complementary organizational traits. From a managerial perspective, our results provide guidance on how to achieve optimal distinctiveness while evolving into more mature market actors. Managers need to consider that although they may have found a favorable degree of distinctiveness when entering the overall market this may change as their status or reputation grows.
